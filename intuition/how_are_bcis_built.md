How are brain-computer interfaces built? How do they work?

For the paper I've chosen to follow, the authors use something called motor imagery (MI). It turns out, parts of our brain associated with movement respond similarly to imagined movements as they do to real movements.
(e.g. raising your right hand vs thinking about raising your right hand leads to similar neural activity). https://lnkd.in/ey5Zgiq7

Naturally, neuroscientists figured this out and put it to use. In the paper, participants were asked to imagine squeezing their left or right hand based on prompts on a screen. They do this many times (120 to be precise). 

The point being? If we have sufficient data of imagined right/left hand squeezing, we can build machine learning models that can differentiate the neural activity associated with them. 

Once we have that, we can basically "deploy" these models in real world use cases. The same imagined movement could be used for controlling a wheel chair (for example, imagine right hand squeeze = turn right, imagine left hand squeeze = turn left and so on). https://lnkd.in/eMUkFNaz

But, as with most things, things are a bit more nuanced. Next, I'll write a bit about more why this process is not straightforward, and what brain "activity" really means in this context.
