Why do brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) fail in real-world settings?

recap: we know that when building BCIs using motor imagery (brain activity coming from imagined movements), participants imagine simple movements many times while their neural activity is recorded. This data is then used to build BCIs that can differentiate different user intentions.

So what's stopping this science fiction like technology from helping everyone in need? 

One of the biggest challenges in this domain (and especially with motor imagery) is a problem called "BCI illiteracy", meaning that BCIs do not work effectively for 15 - 30% of the target populations. (https://lnkd.in/ertJtWMd)

What's interesting is that this stands true for both healthy and impaired populations. Reasons could range from variation of brain structures across individuals to factors related to personality. (https://lnkd.in/e2VzmpMj)

How is this dealt with? There's a plethora of solutions in place for this: i.e., switching to a different experiment type, feature optimization, etc. The paper we are following aimed to address this with using Deep Learning (CNNs) for estimating imagined movement from neural activity.
